Science Research (2018-2020)

Main character from the first game (2D Platformer)

My experience in programming began when I was a sophomore in high school. Before the summer leading into my junior year, I approached the head of the science department and inquired about the SUNY Albany Affiliated High School Science Research Program that some of my classmates were in. I wasn't sure what topic I wanted to research, but I always had an interest in the STEM field. I had a wide array of personal interests, but ultimately I decided on video game development.

The program was rigorous, as it required a lot of time spent outside of class finding and reading peer-reviewed articles, learning how to code (I had no experience going into these projects), and creating a posterboard and presentation. Despite all of this work, I had a lot of fun sitting in the research room during study hall and lunch periods talking with peers as I worked. I made many friends, received a new perspective on the field of computer science, and learned a lot from my peers. I credit this program with fueling my passion for STEM and keeping me motivated to continue learning.

Featured below are each of the two projects that I created while researching video game development. Unfortunately, both projects were destroyed in a hard drive failure on my old laptop, and I failed to store a backup of them anywhere since I didn't intend on pursuing a future in video game development at the time. This is one of the biggest regrets of my life, as I put a lot of time into these projects and thoroughly enjoyed my time working on them. Although the games no longer exist, some fragments of them live on through various screenshots and GIFs I included in my symposium presentations. Had I not randomly decided to log into my old high school Google Drive to take a look around, all of the work I did over those two years may have been completely forgotten. This page contains what remains of my projects.

Junior Year - 2D Platformer Game

The game I worked on for my junior year was a basic 2D platformer. Since I had no experience in programming, I knew that I wouldn't be able to create a game from scratch. I followed YouTube videos to learn how to do pretty much everything. (Credit to Brackeys for guiding me through the process and providing valuable resources for the process.) I would follow alongside the views, learning and creating at the same time. This was not a great way to learn how to program, but it was a good way to learn about the process and effort it takes to create a video game.

The game itself was very straightforward. There were two levels: the first was a practice level that I used to teach myself how to build levels and the second was the level that I showcased during the symposium. Inside each level, the player could control a knight character and navigate the level in an attempt to reach the end. The knight could walk and run left and right, jump, and slide, and could be controlled using either the keyboard or a PlayStation 4 controller. The knight had animation cycles for each of these actions, as shown in one of the images below. There was also an attacking animation which I implemented, but I never got around to adding any enemies to fight. The movement in the game was very fun, in part thanks to a bug that I turned into a feature. The script that controls whether the player is allowed to jump or not could not tell the difference between a wall and a floor. As such, if the knight was pressed up against a wall, the player could jump repeatedly and ascend the wall, gaining vertical velocity with each input. I found it to be extremely fun to launch my character in the air and try to land him on a small platform, so I decided to keep it in. I even constructed part of the showcase level around it.

All of the graphics in the game were created by me, with the exception of the knight character. There were simply too many animations to create and I didn't have the time to create them all, so I found and downloaded a character sprite sheet from UNITY's asset store. For the rest of the graphics, I drew inspiration from pixel art I found online, which is quite apparent when contrasting the quality of the knight with everything else. I also created a song for the game, which played on a loop while running. Once again, this sound file does not exist, but having created the song and listened to it on a loop for hours while play-testing, I will never forget how it goes.

Both of my games were created using the UNITY game engine. As a pure beginner, I was overwhelmed by how expansive UNITY was. If it weren't for the video tutorials I followed, I would have been completely lost. Despite this initial shock, after using it for an extended period of time I became very comfortable with the basic operations for game development within UNITY. If I were to go back to it with my current understanding of programming, I feel confident that I would be able to create a very good game entirely from scratch. Although this game was nothing more than running around in a playground, it was a lot of fun to make and was a great introduction to video game development.

Senior Year - RPG Game

For my second-year game, I wanted to go in a different direction. I was looking through UNITY’s asset store one day and came across "Creator Kit: RPG", an asset bundle that looked really interesting. It contained some character sprites for animations, items to interact with, and a tileset to create the world with, as well as all the scripts necessary to run the game. I downloaded it and began testing it out to see what it was. After a few hours of having fun setting up a little fantasy town, I realized that I could turn this game world into my second project.

From there, I continued to expand the town I had created. Compared to the first game, the world creation in this RPG game was done in a more professional way. Included in the asset bundle was a tileset containing all placeable tiles. These tiles could be joined together to form paths, fields, houses, lakes, and more. Each tile came with its own attributes, such as colliders and opacity. For example, the roof tile could be scaled and stretched to fit the shape of any building, and if the player stepped underneath a roof, it would turn invisible and allow the player to see into the building. I utilized the tileset shown below to construct a decorated, sizable game world for the player to explore.

However, a game needs more than a world to walk around in. Since this is an RPG game, the asset bundle came with a quest feature. I created three quests for the player to process through. The first involved finding the lost axe of an NPC and then collecting some golden apples for him. The second involved retrieving stolen pots that were scattered throughout the world. The third quest was never fully incorporated into the game, but it involved a battle system, which will be explained shortly. Creating and managing these quests were very simple, and a lot of fun to make. Being that this is an RPG game, if there are quests to complete, then an inventory system is a necessity. Thankfully, the asset bundle also came with a built-in inventory system. This feature handled the picking up and management of items in the player's possession. It even could be extended to items brought in from outside the package. The asset bundle was a huge help in the creation of this game.

There were some things that the asset bundle did not contain that I wanted to include in the game, however. In one of the quests, the player needs to find an NPC’s axe. There was no axe asset in the bundle, so I created it myself. Although this wasn’t a major addition, there were two significant features I wanted to add. The first was a menu system containing a pause and main menu. Much like the first game, I still needed to follow along with YouTube videos to learn how to accomplish this (again, huge shoutout to Brackeys), but I was able to get a basic menu system working. The second was a turn-based battle system that I saw in a video by Brackeys. One of my favorite franchises is Pokemon, whose games feature a battle system nearly identical to this. I wanted to have a similar mechanic in my game, so I followed along with the video. However, something was still lacking. The battle system in the video only gave the player two options: fight or heal. I wanted to add a third option, flee, but I had no idea how to go about adding a new button. I spent many hours trying to create that additional button, but eventually, it worked. I will never forget how excited I was when I saw that button show up for the first time and how proud I was of myself for figuring out how to do it on my own. That was one of the moments that made me realize how cool programming could be.
